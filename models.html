Motivation & Models

The science behind the simulator: why plain IDM isn't enough, and two Bayesian extensions — MA-IDM (Gaussian-process driver noise) and dynamic-regression IDM (AR(p) driver noise) — that capture the temporal structure of human driving error.

1. Motivation: why stochastic car-following?

Deterministic car-following models such as the Intelligent Driver Model (IDM) are elegant, parsimonious, and capture the first-order dynamics of traffic flow very well. But when we actually look at real trajectory data — from HighD, NGSIM, or any modern drone survey — individual drivers simply do not drive that cleanly.

Two observations motivate our work:

  1. Residuals are large and persistent. After fitting IDM to a driver's trajectory, the acceleration residual \(\eta_n(t) = \ddot x_n^{\mathrm{obs}}(t) - f_{\mathrm{IDM}}(\cdot)\) has a marginal standard deviation on the order of 0.2 m/s² and an autocorrelation time of 1–2 s. It is not white noise.
  2. Noise changes macroscopic behaviour. Feeding white-noise residuals back into a simulation produces high-frequency jitter with little effect on flow. Feeding correlated residuals — with the same marginal variance — produces persistent stop-and-go waves and measurable fundamental-diagram hysteresis. The colour of the noise matters, not just its amplitude.

This site lets you see that second point directly. The compare page runs three identical ring roads that differ only in the noise model driving the acceleration residual; the differences in ACF(τ), fundamental diagram, and jam-duration statistics are visible within seconds.

Background: the IDM equation

All three stochastic variants here share the same deterministic core — the Intelligent Driver Model of Treiber, Hennecke & Helbing (2000):

\[ \dot v_n \;=\; a\!\left[1 - \left(\frac{v_n}{v_0}\right)^{\delta} - \left(\frac{s^{*}(v_n,\Delta v_n)}{s_n}\right)^{2}\right], \qquad s^{*}(v,\Delta v) \;=\; s_0 + v\,T + \frac{v\,\Delta v}{2\sqrt{a\,b}}. \]

The parameters are: desired speed \(v_0\), safe time headway \(T\), minimum gap \(s_0\), max acceleration \(a\), comfortable braking \(b\), and acceleration exponent \(\delta\). The stochastic extensions below all modify the acceleration as

\[ \dot v_n(t) \;=\; f_{\mathrm{IDM}}\!\big(v_n, \Delta v_n, s_n\big) \;+\; \eta_n(t), \]

and the only difference between the three models is the stochastic process we assume for \(\eta_n(t)\).

2. MA-IDM — Memory-Augmented IDM (Gaussian-process noise)

Zhang & Sun (2024), Bayesian Calibration of the Intelligent Driver Model

IEEE Transactions on Intelligent Transportation Systems · doi:10.1109/TITS.2024.3354102 · [arXiv:2210.03571]

Key idea. Model the residual \(\eta_n(t)\) as a zero-mean Gaussian process with a stationary covariance kernel \(k(t,t')\). The GP endows the residual with memory: how strongly \(\eta_n(t)\) depends on \(\eta_n(t-\tau)\) is controlled entirely by the kernel.

\[ \eta_n(t) \,\sim\, \mathcal{GP}\!\big(0,\; k(t,t')\big), \qquad k(\tau) \;=\; \sigma_k^{2}\, \kappa\!\left(\frac{|\tau|}{\ell}\right). \]

The simulator exposes four kernels (RBF and Matérn 5/2, 3/2, 1/2), all with the same marginal variance \(\sigma_k^{2}\) and lengthscale \(\ell\). Smoother kernels (RBF) produce smoother acceleration noise; rougher kernels (Matérn 1/2, the Ornstein–Uhlenbeck limit) produce visibly jittery traces with the same long-run variance.

Calibrated values. On the HighD dataset the paper reports posterior-mean \(\sigma_k \approx 0.202~\mathrm{m/s^2}\) and \(\ell \approx 1.44~\mathrm{s}\), which are used as the simulator's defaults for the GP mode.

Simulation. Direct sampling from a GP is \(O(N^3)\) in the trajectory length, so we use a random-Fourier-feature approximation \(\eta(t) \approx \sigma\sqrt{2/M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\cos(\omega_m t + b_m)\) with frequencies drawn from the kernel's spectral density. This keeps sampling \(O(M)\) per step while preserving the target covariance.

3. Dynamic-regression IDM — AR(p) noise

Zhang, Wang & Sun (2024), Calibrating Car-Following Models via Bayesian Dynamic Regression

Transportation Research Part C (ISTTT25) · doi:10.1016/j.trc.2024.104719 · [arXiv:2307.03340]

Key idea. Instead of a continuous-time GP, assume the discretised residual follows an autoregressive process of order \(p\):

\[ \eta_t \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{p} \rho_i\,\eta_{t-i} \;+\; \varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}\!\big(0,\, \sigma_\varepsilon^{2}\big). \]

The coefficients \(\{\rho_1,\ldots,\rho_p\}\) are inferred jointly with the IDM parameters in a fully Bayesian framework. The paper selects model order via DIC/WAIC on HighD and reports posterior-mean \(\boldsymbol\rho\) vectors for \(p=1,\ldots,7\); the simulator uses those exact values (Table 1 of the paper, 5 Hz sampling).

Why AR? AR(p) is the discrete-time analogue of a low-order linear SDE driving \(\eta(t)\). It is cheap to simulate (\(O(p)\) per step, no matrix factorisations), exactly stationary for any admissible \(\boldsymbol\rho\), and the coefficients themselves are interpretable — \(\rho_1\) is the lag-1 autocorrelation, the dominant root of the characteristic polynomial sets the memory timescale, and so on.

On the benchmark data, AR(p) achieves predictive log-likelihood comparable to the GP while being roughly an order of magnitude faster to fit, making it attractive for real-time and online calibration.

4. White-noise baseline (B-IDM)

For reference, the simulator also includes the simplest Bayesian IDM formulation, in which the residual is assumed to be i.i.d. Gaussian:

\[ \eta_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^{2}), \qquad \mathrm{Cov}(\eta_t,\eta_{t'}) \;=\; \sigma^{2}\,\mathbb{1}[t=t']. \]

This is what falls out of a naive least-squares or Gaussian-likelihood calibration. It is a useful baseline precisely because its failure modes — washed-out jam waves, unrealistic short-timescale oscillation — are exactly what the two correlated models above are designed to fix.
